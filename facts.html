Learn the facts. top ↑

Depression is a medical condition.

  • It's not a choice: depression changes how we think and feel.1
  • Depression isn't just sadness— it can feel like lasting emptiness.1
  • Neuroanatomy plays a role in depression,1 as do genetics.6
  • Depressed brains are often physically different from non-depressed brains.2

Depression impacts nyc.

  • Major depressive disorder is the greatest source of disability in NYC.3
  • 8% of men and 10% of women in nyc live with depression.3
  • Depression contributes to $14 billion in annual losses, citywide.4
  • At least 1 in 5 new yorkers will experience a mental health disorder this year.3

Depression can affect anyone.

  • Anyone can experience depression, even "successful" people.
  • Women in the U.S. are twice as likely to experience depression.3
  • Some jobs increase the risk (especially real estate, law, and transit).5
  • Underserved communities are 2-3x more vulnerable and less likely to get help.3

Depression is treatable.

  • 80% of people in treatment see improvement in 4-6 weeks.7
  • … But two thirds of new yorkers don't get treatment.3
  • Younger new yorkers are about half as likely to seek help.3
  • Stigma and embarassment are major obstacles to getting care.8

Stigma is serious.

  • Stigma has serious negative effects on hope and self-esteem.9
  • Misunderstandings about depression increase isolation.
  • … And isolation can make depression symptoms much worse.10
  • Stigma is often internalized, making it harder to seek help.11

Learn the symptoms. top ↑

Depression is more than sadness.

People with depression may experience several of these symptoms for an extended period of time.

Emotional

  • Feeling numb, empty, or sad.
  • Feeling guilty or worthless.
  • Feeling hopeless or pessimistic.
  • Feeling anxious or angry.

Physical

  • Lasting fatigue or low energy.
  • Problems with sleep.
  • Changes in weight or appetite.
  • Trouble remembering or focusing.

Lifestyle

  • Isolating yourself.
  • Losing interest in hobbies.
  • Avoiding friends and family.
  • Difficulty making decisions.

Urgent

  • Thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Thoughts of self-harm.
  • Physical pain.
  • Click here for help.

Men and women may have different symptoms.1

  • Women are more likely to feel sadness, worthlessness, and guilt.
  • Men are more likely to feel tired, irritable, and to lose interest in activities.

Learn about treatment. top ↑

Depression is treatable.

There are many ways to treat depression, with new options currently being researched.

Note that this isn't medical advice, just a list of some options.

First steps

Tell someone
Reaching out can be hard, but it's often the best thing you can do.
Learn more
Get medical care
A psychiatrist, psychologist, or primary care doctor can help put together a treatment plan.
Learn more
NYC well
NYC offers a free, confidential service that can help you take the first steps— by phone or text.
Learn more

Next steps

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
CBT is a highly effective12 type of talk therapy that helps change your thoughts by adjusting behaviors.
Learn more
Talk therapy
There are many types of talk therapy that help treat depression. Both one-on-one and group counseling can really help.
Learn more
Physical exam
Depression can be caused by diet13, hormones14, or a number of medical conditions. It's important to rule these out.
Learn more

Lifestyle changes

Eat healthy
Issues with your diet can lead to depression.13 Your doctor can help identify any deficiencies.
Learn more
Get exercise
Aerobic exercise like taking a walk or hitting the gym can give reduce depression.15
Learn more
Mindfulness
Mindfulness is a way of thinking that can help rewire your brain.16 Mindfulness-based therapy can cut the risk of relapse in half.17
Learn more

Medication

Antidepressants
There are many types of antidepressants available and they really do help.18 Your doctor can help you find the best option.
Learn more
Genetic testing
New genetic tests can help you and your doctor choose an effective medication and dosage.19
Learn more
Ketamine
Ketamine is an effective20 new option for treatment-resistent depression that works quickly.
Learn more

More options

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)
A simple, in-office treatment that provides 50-60%21 of patients with relief.
Learn more
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)
ECT is a highly effective,22 in-patient option for severe, treatment-resistent depression.
Learn more
Electrostimulation therapy
Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS) and deep brain stimulation (DBS) can help treat certain types of depression.
Learn more

New research

Psylocibin
The FDA recently granted psylocibin "breakthrough therapy" status after a successful feasibility study.23
Learn more
Microdosing
Initial research shows that small (i.e., non-hallucinatory) doses of psychedelics can help relieve depression.24
Learn more
Anti-inflammatory medicine
Several studies have identified a promising link between depression and inflammation.25
Learn more
Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT)
An early study found that following FMT, 30% of patients reported a mental health improvement.26
Learn more

Feeling overwhelmed? Call or text NYC Well for confidential help.
