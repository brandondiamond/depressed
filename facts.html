Learn the facts. top ↑

Depression is a medical condition.

  • It's not a choice: depression changes how we think and feel.1
  • Depression isn't just sadness— it can feel like lasting emptiness.1
  • Neuroanatomy plays a role in depression,1 as do genetics.5
  • Depressed brains are often physically different from non-depressed brains.2

Depression impacts nyc.

  • Major depressive disorder is the greatest source of disability in NYC.3
  • 8% of men and 10% of women in nyc live with depression.3
  • Depression contributes to $14 billion in annual losses, citywide.4
  • At least 1 in 5 new yorkers will experience a mental health disorder this year.3

Depression can affect anyone.

  • Anyone can experience depression, even "successful" people.
  • Women in the U.S. are twice as likely to experience depression.3
  • Some jobs increase the risk (especially real estate, law, and transit).5
  • Underserved communities are 2-3x more vulnerable and less likely to get help.3

Depression is treatable.

  • 80% of people in treatment see improvement in 4-6 weeks.6
  • … But two thirds of new yorkers don't get treatment.3
  • Younger new yorkers are about half as likely to seek help.3
  • Stigma and embarassment are major obstacles to getting care.7

Stigma is serious.

  • Stigma has serious negative effects on hope and self-esteem.8
  • Misunderstandings about depression increase isolation.
  • … And isolation can make depression symptoms much worse.9
  • Stigma is often internalized, making it harder to seek help.10

Learn the symptoms. top ↑

Depression is more than sadness.

People with depression may experience several of these symptoms for an extended period of time.

Emotional

  • Feeling numb, empty, or sad.
  • Feeling guilty or worthless.
  • Feeling hopeless or pessimistic.
  • Feeling anxious or angry.

Physical

  • Lasting fatigue or low energy.
  • Problems with sleep.
  • Changes in weight or appetite.
  • Trouble remembering or focusing.

Lifestyle

  • Isolating yourself.
  • Losing interest in hobbies.
  • Avoiding friends and family.
  • Difficulty making decisions.

Urgent

  • Thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Thoughts of self-harm.
  • Physical pain.
  • These are serious concerns.

Men and women may have different symptoms.1

  • Women are more likely to feel sadness, worthlessness, and guilt.
  • Men are more likely to feel tired, irritable, and to lose interest in activities.

Learn about treatment. top ↑

Depression is treatable.

There are many ways to treat depression, with new options currently being researched.

Note that this isn't medical advice, just a list of some options.

First steps

Tell someone
It can be hard to reach out, but having an ally can really help.
Learn more
Get medical care
A doctor, psychiatrist, or psychologist will help you find a treatment that works.

Next steps

Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
CBT is a type of talk therapy that helps you change your thoughts by adjusting behaviors.
Learn more
Talk therapy
Though CBT is the "gold standard," any talk therapy (even in a group setting) can really help.
Learn more
Physical exam
Depression can be caused by diet, hormones, or even your thyroid. It's important to rule out these causes.
Learn more

Lifestyle changes

Eat healthy
Nutritional deficiencies can lead to depression. Eating healthy can help.
Learn more
Get exercise
Research has shown that exercise is a healthy way to treat depression.
Learn more
Mindfulness
Meditation and mindfulness have been shown to help ease depression.
Learn more

Medication

Antidepressants
There are several types of antidepressants that help treat depression. Your doctor can find the best one for you.
Learn more
Genetic testing
New genetic tests can help you and your doctor choose an effective medication and dosage.
Learn more
Ketamine
Ketamine is a new option for treatment-resistent depression that can work quickly.
Learn more

More options

Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)
A non-invasive treatment (rTMS or dTMS) that directly stimulates the cortex, with 30-40% of patients experiencing relief.
Learn more
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)
An in-patient treatment for severe depression that helps many achieve remission.
Learn more
Electrostimulation therapy
Vagus nerve stimulation (VNS) and deep brain stimulation (DBS) may be appropriate for some people.
Learn more

New research

Psylocibin
The FDA recently granted psylocibin "breakthrough therapy" status for treating depression.
Learn more
Microdosing
Scientists are researching the effectiveness of "micro" doses of psychedelics for treating depression.
Learn more
Anti-inflammatory medicine
Early research indicates that anti-inflammatory medicine may help treat depression.
Learn more
Fecal microbiota transplant (FMT)
There is increasing evidence that gut bacteria can help prevent depression.
